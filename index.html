Communication Skills Overview

Communication is a multifaceted process involving the exchange of information, ideas, emotions, and meaning between individuals or groups.

It's more than just the transfer of words; it encompasses a deep understanding of the sender's intent, the receiver's interpretation, and the context in which the message is conveyed.

1. Learning Objectives

  • Basic Principles of Communication: Understanding the foundational elements that make communication effective.
  • Communication Process: Exploring how communication occurs, including the roles of different participants.
  • Types of Communication: Identifying various forms of communication and their appropriate contexts.

2. Basic Principles of Communication (The 7 C's)

  1. Clarity:
    • The message should be clear and easily understandable.
    • Avoid jargon and complex language.
    • Example: Use simple language when explaining technical concepts.
  2. Conciseness:
    • Communication should be brief and to the point.
    • Avoid unnecessary details.
    • Example: Focus on key findings in a business report.
  3. Completeness:
    • A complete message provides all necessary information.
    • Answer potential questions the audience may have.
    • Example: Include date, time, location, and agenda in meeting invitations.
  4. Correctness:
    • Accuracy in communication is vital.
    • Use proper grammar, spelling, and punctuation.
    • Example: A well-proofread document reflects professionalism.
  5. Courtesy:
    • Communication should be respectful and polite.
    • Be mindful of tone and language.
    • Example: Use "please" and "thank you."
  6. Consideration:
    • Tailor your message to the audience's needs and expectations.
    • Understand the background and knowledge level of your audience.
    • Example: Use more technical language with experts than with a general audience.
  7. Concrete:
    • Make your message specific and tangible.
    • Provide examples, evidence, or visuals.
    • Example: Use statistics or case studies to strengthen your argument.

3. Communication Process

  1. Sender: The individual or entity that initiates the communication.
  2. Message: The information, idea, or thought being conveyed.
  3. Medium: The channel through which the message is transmitted (e.g., spoken word, written text).
  4. Receiver: The individual or group who receives and interprets the message.
  5. Feedback: The response from the receiver back to the sender.

4. Special Needs Communication

  1. Deaf and Hard of Hearing: Use sign language or written communication.
  2. Blind and Visually Impaired: Utilize Braille or audio formats.
  3. Illiterate Individuals: Employ visual aids, pictures, or symbols.
  4. Elderly: Provide large print materials.
  5. Culturally Diverse Audiences: Use universally recognized signs and symbols.

5. Types of Communication

  • Written Communication: Reports, publications, letters.
  • Oral Communication: Conversations, presentations, discussions.
  • Special Communication for Special Groups: Methods for communicating with individuals who have specific needs.
  • Action-Oriented Communication: Drama, music, role plays.

Barriers in coommunication

A barrier: This is broadly defined as to any factor or obstacle that hinders the effective communication.

These barriers can prevent the intended message from being properly understood, resulting in miscommunication or a breakdown in communication.

    Barriers of effective communication include;

  1. Physical Barriers:
    2. These obstacles prevent or disrupt communication, such as distance, noise, or even closed doors. These barriers can make it challenging for individuals to hear, see, or interact with each other effectively.
  2. Language Barriers:
    3. Differences in language and linguistic abilities can create difficulties in understanding and conveying messages. This barrier can occur when people speak different languages or have different levels of proficiency in a shared language.
  3. Psychological Barriers:
    4. These relate to the internal factors that can impact communication. Include personal biases, preconceived notions, and lack of attention or interest, emotional states i.e. anger, fear in the message being conveyed.
  4. Cultural Barriers:
    5. Values, beliefs, norms, and behaviors, can impede communication. Different cultural backgrounds may lead to misinterpretations, misunderstandings, or offensive remarks if not considered and understood.
  5. Organizational Barriers:
    6. These stem from the structure, policies, and practices within an organization that may hinder effective communication. They include hierarchical structures, poor leadership, insufficient channels of communication, and a lack of feedback mechanisms.
  6. Technological Barriers
    7. : Advancements in technology have brought new communication tools and platforms, but they can also create barriers. Technical issues, unfamiliarity with technology, or limited access to communication devices can hinder effective communication.
  7. Semantic Barriers:
    8. These arise when there are differences in the meanings assigned to words, symbols, or gestures. These differences can lead to misunderstandings, as individuals may interpret messages differently based on their own understanding and context.
  8. Lack of subject knowledge:
    9. if the sender lacks subject knowledge on the message he's conveying then this could result into misinterpretation of the message by the receiver.

    Other barriers include

  9. Long difficult words
  10. Unfamiliar words
  11. Long sessions with no breaks
  12. Useless and unnecessary words
  13. Beating about the bush
  14. Poor body language
  15. Spelling and gramma errors
